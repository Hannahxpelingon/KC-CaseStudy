01 — Overview

Bridging the gap between a great food truck and its customers

KC's Fresh Fruit & Smoothies is a popular food truck located at 33rd St. & Lancaster Walk, serving the Drexel University community. Despite a loyal following, KC's had no digital ordering presence — customers relied entirely on in-person interactions with no way to browse the menu, customize orders, or skip long wait times.

Team 4Corners took on the challenge across two consecutive courses. In IDM215, the team designed a Figma prototype that envisioned what a mobile ordering experience could look like for KC's. In IDM216, we picked up where that left off: revamping the interface design, conducting two rounds of user testing (alpha on the Figma prototype and beta on the coded version), and ultimately building a fully functional HTML/CSS web app deployed to production.

My contributions spanned the entire lifecycle — I served as secondary project manager, led the visual redesign of the interface, and was the primary person responsible for coding the final product in HTML and CSS.

02 — Context & Challenge

A food truck with no digital presence

KC's Smoothie operates as a cash-and-carry food truck with no app, no website, and no way for customers to place orders in advance. During peak hours — between classes, at lunch, and during campus events — lines grow long and potential customers walk away rather than wait.

🏗️

Background

This project is the second phase of a two-course sequence. IDM215 produced a Figma prototype; IDM216 extended that work into a tested, coded, and deployed web application.

⚠️

The Problem

KC's had zero digital infrastructure. No menu online, no ordering system, no way to reduce wait times or reach customers outside of the physical truck location.

🎯

Goals & Objectives

Design and build a usable, accessible mobile ordering interface. Validate the design through two rounds of testing. Deliver a live, coded product — not just a prototype.

👥

Target Audience

Anyone in the KC's community: Drexel students, local residents, faculty and staff, and nearby workers such as construction workers frequenting the area.

Target Audience Segments

Our research identified four primary groups whose needs shaped the design — from quick, on-the-go orders for students between classes, to a familiar and easy-to-navigate interface for non-student regulars.

03 — Process & Insight

From research to redesign to real code

Our process spanned two courses and followed a disciplined design-test-iterate cycle. Below is a walkthrough of each major phase, with notes on my specific contributions at each stage.

🔍

Target Audience & User Personas

The team identified four audience segments that reflect the real makeup of KC's customer base. Personas were developed to ground design decisions — a busy Drexel student who wants to order ahead between classes looks very different from a construction worker on a lunch break who may be a first-time user. These personas guided everything from the navigation structure to the copy on the menu screens.

04 — The Solution

A clean, mobile-first ordering experience

The final product is a fully coded, deployed web application that gives KC's customers a way to browse the menu, customize their order, and manage their bag — all from their phone. The interface was designed to feel approachable and quick for users on the go, with a clear visual hierarchy that makes scanning the menu and placing an order effortless.

Personalized Home Screen

A persistent bag icon and an order history tab allow users to track current and past orders — reducing anxiety around whether an order was placed and making repeat ordering frictionless.

Every design and code decision was made with a mobile screen in mind. Navigation is thumb-friendly, tap targets are generously sized, and the layout adapts naturally to smaller viewports.

05 — Results

From zero digital presence to a live product

The project successfully delivered on its core objective: taking KC's from having no digital presence whatsoever to a fully coded, deployed, and user-tested web application. Two rounds of usability testing with real users validated the design direction and informed meaningful iterations at each stage.

🧪 Two-Stage Testing Process

Running alpha testing on the Figma prototype and beta testing on the coded build gave us two distinct feedback loops. Issues caught at the prototype stage were resolved before a single line of code was written, saving significant development time.

🚀 Live, Deployed Product

The final product is deployed on Vercel and publicly accessible — a genuine deliverable, not just a classroom exercise. KC's now has a working digital interface it can build on.

🎨 Design-to-Code Fidelity

One of the biggest challenges was maintaining visual consistency between the Figma designs and the coded output. By owning both the redesign and the implementation, I was able to ensure the final product closely reflected the intended design intent.

💡 Lessons Learned

If I were to approach this project again, I would invest more time in establishing design tokens and a shared CSS variable system from the very beginning of development — not just for consistency, but to make iterating on testing feedback much faster. I would also advocate for testing with a broader and more diverse group of users earlier in the process, particularly targeting the non-student segments (faculty, nearby workers) whose use patterns differ meaningfully from a typical Drexel student. Finally, the transition from Figma to code always surfaces edge cases the prototype can't predict — building in dedicated time for this "translation phase" would have made the process smoother.
